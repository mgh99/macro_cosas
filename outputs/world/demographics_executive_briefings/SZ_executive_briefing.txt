**Structural Consumer Base & Demographic Momentum**
The market exhibits a **structurally expanding consumer base** with total population growth averaging 1.5% annually over the past decade, supported by consistent natural increase (19.2k–20.1k annually) and partially offset by net migration outflows (-4.2k to -12.5k). The 10-year projection confirms this trajectory, with population rising from 1.21B (2022) to 1.38B (2036), driven by sustained fertility and moderating migration losses. However, the **core consumption cohort (25–49)** is shrinking as a share of the total population (from 17.5% in 2021 to 16.8% in 2023), while the working-age population (15–64) remains stable at ~65%. Accelerated ageing is evident in the 65+ cohort, growing at 4.2% CAGR, with the 80+ segment expanding by 6.1% annually. This suggests **migration-supported equilibrium** with structural risks: declining core cohort share may constrain mass-market demand, while ageing dynamics could shift spending toward premium/healthcare categories. Labor availability remains robust, but long-term demand resilience hinges on migration stability and productivity gains.

**Household Structure & Consumption Architecture**
Average household size has contracted from 4.64 (2006) to 4.04 (2014), signaling a shift toward smaller, individualized households. This trend, combined with ageing, implies a structural rebalancing toward **premium, discretionary consumption** over broad-based mass demand. Smaller households (likely urban, dual-income) favor high-margin, individualized products, while ageing may reduce household spending power. The data suggests a **narrowing consumption base** over time, with declining family-driven demand and rising reliance on niche, experiential, or health-oriented luxury segments. Retail strategies should prioritize **compact, high-value formats** and digital-first engagement to align with these structural shifts. Long-term brand penetration will depend on adapting to ageing demographics and smaller household dynamics.
