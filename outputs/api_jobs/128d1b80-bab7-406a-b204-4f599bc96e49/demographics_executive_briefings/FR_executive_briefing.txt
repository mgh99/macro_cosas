**Structural Consumer Base & Demographic Momentum**
The consumer base exhibits a structurally declining demographic trajectory, with total population growth decelerating from 0.3% CAGR (2016–2023) to near stagnation (0.1% CAGR projected 2024–2036). Natural population change has collapsed from +155.9k in 2016 to a projected +2.9k by 2026, indicating severe fertility decline. Migration remains the sole driver of growth, averaging +110k annually, but this dependency masks underlying contraction. The core consumption cohort (25–49) peaked in 2021 and is projected to shrink by 5–7% over the next decade, while the 65+ cohort grows by 15–20%. The working-age share (15–64) is projected to decline from 60% to 55%, accelerating ageing pressures. This suggests a structurally narrowing demand base, with migration-dependent equilibrium masking long-term contraction. Implications include reduced labor availability, declining mass-market resilience, and heightened reliance on premium segments to sustain revenue growth.

**Household Structure & Consumption Architecture**
Household size has contracted from 2.25 in 2011 to 2.22 in 2015, signaling a shift toward smaller, individualized consumption units. Combined with ageing demographics, this trend implies a structural rebalancing toward premium, discretionary spending over broad-based mass consumption. The shrinking core cohort (25–49) and expanding 65+ population suggest demand will concentrate in urban, high-income segments, while traditional family-driven markets weaken. Retail strategies must prioritize smaller-format stores, experiential luxury, and digital-first engagement to align with these structural shifts. Long-term brand penetration will depend on capturing ageing, affluent households while mitigating exposure to declining mass-market segments.
