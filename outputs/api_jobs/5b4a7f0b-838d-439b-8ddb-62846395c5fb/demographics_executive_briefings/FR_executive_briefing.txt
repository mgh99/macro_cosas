**Structural Consumer Base & Demographic Momentum**
The market exhibits a **structurally declining demographic fundamentals** trajectory, with total population growth decelerating from 0.3% CAGR (2016–2023) to near stagnation (0.1% CAGR projected 2023–2036). Natural population change has collapsed from +155.9k in 2016 to +8.1k in 2026, while net migration remains volatile but critical, accounting for 80% of recent growth. The core consumption cohort (25–49) has plateaued, representing 24% of the population in 2023, while the working-age share (15–64) is shrinking from 62% to an estimated 58% by 2036. Accelerated ageing is evident, with the 65+ cohort growing at 1.2% CAGR and the 80+ segment expanding by 2.1% annually. The 10-year projection confirms a **migration-dependent equilibrium**, with structural risks of labour shortages and demand contraction in key apparel/luxury segments. Long-term retail expansion faces headwinds, requiring targeted strategies for ageing consumers and labour-intensive operations.

**Household Structure & Consumption Architecture**
The market is undergoing **ageing and smaller-household rebalancing**, with average household size declining from 2.25 (2011) to 2.22 (2015) and likely continuing downward. This trend, combined with a shrinking core cohort, suggests a shift toward individualized, premium consumption rather than broad-based mass demand. Smaller households (1–2 persons) now dominate, implying higher discretionary spending intensity but lower per-capita demand for family-oriented apparel. The ageing population further pressures this dynamic, as older, urbanized households prioritize quality over quantity. Store formats should pivot toward experiential, high-margin luxury offerings, while assortments must adapt to smaller, single-person wardrobes. Long-term brand penetration hinges on capturing ageing, affluent demographics through localized, service-driven retail strategies.
