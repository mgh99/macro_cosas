**1. Structural Consumer Base & Demographic Momentum**
The market exhibits structurally declining demographic fundamentals, with total population projected to shrink by 2.2% over the next decade, driven by persistent negative natural change and decelerating net migration. The core consumption cohort (25–49) is contracting, with the 30–34 and 35–39 age groups declining by 2.0% and 5.2% respectively over the past three years, while the 65+ cohort grows at a 3.5% CAGR. The working-age population (15–64) is stabilizing but aging, with the 50–54 and 55–59 cohorts expanding by 1.6% and 3.4% annually. Migration remains critical to offset natural decline, but its contribution is shrinking (CAGR of -12.3% in net inflows). The 10-year projection confirms a structurally ageing and shrinking consumer base, with implications for long-term retail demand resilience, labor availability, and expansion viability. Strategic focus must shift toward demand preservation rather than growth, with heightened sensitivity to migration policy risks.

**2. Household Structure & Consumption Architecture**
Household size has declined from 2.69 in 2011 to 2.58 in 2022, signaling a structural shift toward smaller, individualized consumption units. Combined with ageing demographics, this trend suggests a rebalancing toward premium, discretionary spending rather than broad-based mass consumption. The 40–44 cohort’s 6.0% contraction and 25–29 cohort’s 2.4% decline further weaken traditional family-driven demand. The market now structurally favors individualized consumption expansion, though with constrained growth potential. Retail strategies should prioritize urban, high-margin formats and experiential luxury over volume-driven mass-market approaches, while anticipating reduced labor pool resilience due to ageing. Long-term brand penetration will hinge on adapting to smaller, older households with higher disposable income but lower population growth.
