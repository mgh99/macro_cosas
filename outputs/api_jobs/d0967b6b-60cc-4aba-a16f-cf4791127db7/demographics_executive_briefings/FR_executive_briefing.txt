**Structural Consumer Base & Demographic Momentum**
The market exhibits a **migration-supported equilibrium** with structurally declining natural population growth, as evidenced by the near-zero natural change by 2026 and sustained reliance on net migration (averaging ~100k annually). Total population growth is projected to slow to 0.1% CAGR over the next decade, signaling limited organic demand expansion. The core consumption cohort (25–49) remains stable but shows early signs of contraction, while the working-age share (15–64) is projected to decline marginally. Accelerated ageing is evident in the 65+ cohort, which grows at 1.2% CAGR, and the 80+ segment expands at 2.1% CAGR, intensifying long-term dependency pressures. These trends suggest **structurally constrained demand resilience**, with retail expansion dependent on migration inflows and premiumization strategies targeting ageing consumers. Labor availability may tighten as the working-age population ages, requiring automation or skilled migration to offset productivity risks.

**Household Structure & Consumption Architecture**
Household size has contracted from 2.25 (2011) to 2.22 (2022), reflecting a shift toward smaller, individualized consumption units. Combined with ageing demographics, this implies a structural rebalancing toward **premium, discretionary spending** over mass-market demand. The decline in household size (–1.3% CAGR) aligns with urbanization and later-life single-person households, reducing broad-based consumption elasticity. The market structurally favors **individualized consumption expansion**, necessitating store formats optimized for smaller, high-margin transactions and assortments tailored to ageing, urbanized consumers. Long-term brand penetration will hinge on adapting to these demographic shifts, with risks if migration slows or ageing accelerates further.
