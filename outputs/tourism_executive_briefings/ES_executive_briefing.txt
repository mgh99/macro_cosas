**Tourism Volume & Composition**
The tourism demand base is large and internationally diversified, with total arrivals growing significantly over the past five years, particularly from 2021 to 2024. Foreign arrivals (322.2 million nights in 2024) outpace domestic demand (182.9 million nights), indicating strong international exposure. The origin markets are broadly distributed, with notable contributions from the EU (31.8 million arrivals in 2024), the US (4.5 million), and China (1.3 million), though the latter shows volatility. Growth is broad-based rather than concentrated in specific countries, reducing dependency risks. This suggests a structurally large and internationally diversified demand base, which aligns with exposure to global economic cycles but mitigates concentration risks.

**Seasonality & Stability**
The seasonality ratio has declined from 50% in 2021 to 36% in 2024, classifying the market as moderately seasonal. This reduction indicates a shift toward more stable demand distribution, though peak periods still drive disproportionate revenue. Retail demand is likely cyclically concentrated, with operational implications for inventory planning and staffing. While the trend toward lower seasonality is favorable, peak months remain critical for revenue, necessitating agile retail strategies to balance peak and off-peak performance. The market is transitioning from highly peak-dependent to cyclically concentrated, improving stability but requiring continued adaptability.

**Economic Impact & Spending Quality**
Average expenditure per trip is not provided, but the data suggests a mix of high-frequency (1–3 nights: 188.4 million nights in 2024) and extended stays (4+ nights: 467.8 million nights), indicating a blend of leisure and business travel. Personal trips dominate (140.9 million in 2024), while business travel is stable but lower in volume. The structure supports stable mid-market consumption, with potential for premium spending during extended stays. However, without expenditure data, the exact spending quality remains uncertain. The tourism base is strategically relevant for apparel and luxury retail, particularly in urban and leisure hubs, though further insights into expenditure would refine its premium potential.
