**1. Tourism Volume & Composition**
The tourism demand base is large and internationally diversified, with total arrivals growing from 34.6 million in 2020 to 116.2 million in 2024, reflecting a strong post-pandemic recovery. Foreign arrivals (322.2 million nights in 2024) now exceed domestic (182.9 million), indicating high international exposure. Key markets like the EU27, the UK, and the US show broad-based growth, though China and the US exhibit the most significant year-over-year increases. While Europe dominates (101.1 million arrivals in 2024), the diversification of origin markets—including Asia, Africa, and the Americas—reduces concentration risks. This structure suggests resilience to regional demand shocks, though reliance on intra-EU travel (86.1 million arrivals in 2024) poses some geopolitical exposure. Retail demand is thus broadly supported but vulnerable to global economic cycles.

**2. Seasonality & Stability**
Tourism demand has shifted from highly seasonal (2020 ratio: 54.8%) to moderately seasonal (2024 ratio: 36.0%), with peak periods now contributing less than 40% of annual nights. This trend toward stability reduces operational volatility, though retail demand remains cyclically concentrated, with peak months likely driving disproportionate revenue. The decline in seasonality suggests improved inventory planning and staffing stability, but retailers must still prepare for concentrated demand during key periods. The shift away from extreme seasonality implies a more balanced revenue stream, though peak-dependent retail strategies may still be necessary to capitalize on high-traffic months.

**3. Economic Impact & Spending Quality**
Average expenditure per trip is not provided, but the mix of personal (140.9 million trips in 2024) and business travel (8.6 million) suggests a balanced demand structure. The predominance of short stays (1–3 nights: 188.4 million nights in 2024) indicates high-frequency, lower-spend tourism, though extended stays (4+ nights: 467.8 million) support premium retail opportunities. The combination of volume-driven domestic and higher-spending international visitors positions tourism as a mid-market to high-value driver for apparel and luxury retail. However, without expenditure data, the full premium potential remains uncertain. Retailers should target both leisure and business travelers to maximize revenue stability.
