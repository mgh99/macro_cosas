**1. Tourism Volume & Composition**
The tourism demand base is large and internationally diversified, with total arrivals recovering strongly post-pandemic, reaching 116.2 million in 2024—up from 34.6 million in 2020. Foreign arrivals (54.3 million in 2024) now exceed domestic arrivals (54.3 million), indicating a balanced but increasingly international profile. Key markets like the EU27 (31.8 million arrivals in 2024), the UK (11.3 million), and the US (4.5 million) dominate, though growth is broad-based, with notable contributions from Asia (3.7 million) and Africa (0.9 million). The share of intra-EU arrivals (86.1 million) underscores regional integration, while non-EU markets like the US and China exhibit high growth rates (e.g., China’s arrivals surged 440% from 2020 to 2024). This diversification mitigates exposure to single-market shocks but requires tailored retail strategies to cater to varied cultural preferences. The demand base is thus structurally large and internationally diversified, offering resilience to global demand cycles.

**2. Seasonality & Stability**
The seasonality ratio has declined from 54.8% in 2020 to 36.0% in 2024, classifying the market as moderately seasonal. This shift suggests a more stable demand distribution, though peak periods (e.g., summer months) likely still drive disproportionate revenue. The reduced concentration implies lower volatility for retail demand, though inventory and staffing must still align with seasonal peaks. Operational planning should prioritize flexibility to capitalize on high-traffic months while maintaining service levels during off-peak periods. The structural shift toward moderate seasonality enhances revenue stability, but peak-dependent retail strategies remain critical for maximizing yield.

**3. Economic Impact & Spending Quality**
Average expenditure per trip has not been provided, but the data suggests a high-value tourism structure. Extended stays (4+ nights) accounted for 71.2% of total nights in 2024, up from 68.5% in 2019, indicating a shift toward higher-spending, leisure-focused travelers. Personal travel (140.9 million trips in 2024) dominates over business travel (8.6 million), signaling strong discretionary spending potential. The mix of long-stay, high-expenditure tourists supports premium retail demand, particularly in apparel and luxury segments. The absence of expenditure data limits precise conclusions, but the structural trends—longer stays and leisure-driven demand—point to a market conducive to high-value discretionary spending. This aligns well with luxury retail strategies targeting affluent, experience-oriented travelers.
