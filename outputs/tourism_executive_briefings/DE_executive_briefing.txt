**1. Tourism Volume & Composition**
The tourism demand base is large and internationally diversified, with total arrivals growing from 72.5 million in 2020 to 146.2 million in 2024, reflecting a strong post-pandemic recovery. Domestic travelers dominate (78% of total nights in 2024), but foreign arrivals have rebounded sharply, particularly from Europe (e.g., Switzerland, Netherlands, and the UK), Asia (China, South Korea), and North America (US, Canada). While Europe remains the largest source region, the diversification of origin markets—including growth from Africa, Central/South America, and Oceania—reduces concentration risk. However, reliance on intra-EU travel (46% of foreign arrivals in 2024) exposes the market to regional economic fluctuations. The demand structure is thus large and internationally diversified, though with notable domestic dominance, implying resilience to global shocks but sensitivity to intra-European demand cycles.

**2. Seasonality & Stability**
Tourism demand exhibits moderate seasonality, with the top three months accounting for 30–42% of annual nights (2020–2024). The classification shifted from highly seasonal (2020–2021) to stable (2022–2024), suggesting a structural shift toward more balanced distribution. However, peak periods (e.g., summer) likely drive disproportionate retail revenue, necessitating agile inventory and staffing strategies. The reduced seasonality ratio (30% in 2024) implies lower volatility, but retail demand remains cyclically concentrated, requiring operational flexibility to manage peak surges without overcapacity during off-peak months.

**3. Economic Impact & Spending Quality**
Average expenditure per trip has not been provided, but the data suggests a mixed tourism structure. Personal travel (87% of trips in 2024) dominates, while business travel (13%) contributes to higher-value spending. The length-of-stay distribution reveals a balance between short (1–3 nights, 17% of nights) and extended stays (4+ nights, 83%), indicating potential for both high-frequency and high-value retail opportunities. The absence of expenditure data limits precise conclusions, but the prevalence of longer stays and a significant share of business travel suggests a market conducive to premium discretionary spending, particularly in luxury apparel. However, the lack of expenditure trends precludes definitive assessment of spending quality.
