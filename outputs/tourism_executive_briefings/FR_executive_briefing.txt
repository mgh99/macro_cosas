**1. Tourism Volume & Composition**
The tourism demand base is large and internationally diversified, with total arrivals at tourist accommodation establishments growing significantly over the past five years, particularly from 2021 to 2024. The data shows a strong recovery post-2020, with foreign arrivals increasing from 56.5 million in 2021 to 140.6 million in 2024, while domestic arrivals also expanded, though at a slower pace. The share of foreign tourists (30.7% in 2024) indicates substantial international exposure, with key markets including the US, UK, Germany, and China showing robust growth. However, concentration risks exist, as the top five origin countries (US, UK, Germany, Spain, and Italy) collectively account for a significant portion of arrivals. The demand base is thus large and internationally diversified, though with some dependency on major source markets, exposing retail to global economic cycles and geopolitical risks.

**2. Seasonality & Stability**
Tourism demand exhibits moderate seasonality, with the seasonality ratio (Top 3 months nights / total annual nights) consistently below 35% from 2021 to 2024, classifying the market as stable. This suggests a relatively balanced distribution of tourist activity throughout the year, reducing volatility in retail demand. However, the data indicates a slight decline in seasonality over time, which may reflect a shift toward more evenly distributed travel patterns. While peak periods do not dominate, the stability of demand allows for more predictable inventory planning and staffing, minimizing operational disruptions. Retail performance is thus structurally stable, with limited peak-dependent volatility, supporting consistent revenue streams.

**3. Economic Impact & Spending Quality**
The tourism structure supports high-value discretionary spending, as evidenced by the steady increase in average expenditure per trip over the past five years. The mix of personal (91.5% of trips in 2024) and business travel (8.5%) suggests a strong leisure-driven market, with longer stays (4+ nights accounting for 77.3% of total nights in 2024) indicating higher spending potential. The combination of extended stays and rising per-trip expenditure signals a premium-oriented tourism base, favorable for apparel and luxury retail. The absence of low-margin volume tourism further enhances the strategic relevance of this demand for high-end retail, as tourists are likely to engage in discretionary purchases during their visits.
