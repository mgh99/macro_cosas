**1. Tourism Volume & Composition**
The tourism demand base is large and internationally diversified, with total arrivals growing from 59.7 million in 2020 to 120.2 million in 2024, reflecting a post-pandemic recovery. Domestic travelers account for approximately 68% of total nights spent, while foreign visitors contribute 32%, indicating a balanced but domestically anchored market. The origin markets are broadly diversified, with significant contributions from Europe (43% of foreign arrivals in 2024), North America (19%), and Asia (10%), mitigating concentration risks. However, the strong recovery in European and North American arrivals suggests exposure to global demand cycles, particularly from high-spending regions. The demand structure is thus large and internationally diversified, though domestic demand remains a stabilizing factor.

**2. Seasonality & Stability**
The seasonality ratio has declined from 45.5% in 2020 (highly seasonal) to 31.0% in 2024 (low seasonality), indicating a shift toward a more stable demand pattern. This reduction in peak concentration suggests lower volatility for retail demand, though peak periods may still drive disproportionate revenue during summer months. The operational implications are favorable, with reduced need for extreme inventory or staffing adjustments. However, the residual seasonality requires strategic planning to sustain performance during off-peak months. Overall, tourism-driven retail demand is structurally stable, with diminishing cyclical concentration.

**3. Economic Impact & Spending Quality**
Average expenditure per trip has not been explicitly provided, but the data suggests a mix of high-frequency domestic travel and higher-spending international visitors, particularly from North America and Asia. The share of business travel increased from 8% in 2015 to 14% in 2024, indicating potential for premium spending. The length-of-stay distribution shows a growing share of short stays (1–3 nights), which may limit per-visitor expenditure but supports higher transaction volumes. The tourism structure thus appears to balance stable mid-market consumption with pockets of high-value discretionary spending, particularly from international business and leisure travelers. This supports the strategic relevance of tourism for apparel and luxury retail, though with a focus on volume-driven and mid-tier offerings.
