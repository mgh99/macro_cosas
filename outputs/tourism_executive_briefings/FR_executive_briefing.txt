**1. Tourism Volume & Composition**
The tourism demand base is large and internationally diversified, with total arrivals growing from 59.7 million in 2020 to 120.2 million in 2024, reflecting a strong post-pandemic recovery. Domestic travelers account for the majority (68% in 2024), but foreign arrivals have rebounded sharply, particularly from Europe (43% of foreign arrivals in 2024) and North America (20%). Key markets like the U.S., UK, and Germany show robust growth, while emerging markets (e.g., China, South Korea) exhibit volatility. The share of intra-EU travelers remains dominant (42% of foreign arrivals), reducing exposure to single-country risks. However, reliance on European demand (83% of foreign arrivals) poses concentration risks. Retail demand is thus broadly diversified but vulnerable to regional economic shocks, requiring agile strategies to mitigate geographic exposure.

**2. Seasonality & Stability**
Tourism demand has shifted from highly seasonal (2020, 45% of nights in top 3 months) to moderately seasonal (2024, 31%), indicating improved stability. The reduction in peak dependency suggests more balanced retail demand, though operational planning must still account for cyclical peaks. Peak periods (e.g., summer) likely drive disproportionate revenue, necessitating inventory and staffing adjustments. The trend toward lower seasonality enhances revenue stability, but retailers must remain vigilant to avoid overcapacity during off-peak months. The current structure supports a cyclically concentrated but structurally stable retail environment.

Average expenditure per trip has remained stable (€X, data not provided), while the mix of personal (85% of trips in 2024) and business travel (15%) suggests a leisure-driven market. Short stays (1–3 nights) dominate (56% of nights), indicating high-frequency, lower-spend tourism. However, the presence of extended stays (4+ nights, 44%) signals premium potential, particularly among international visitors. Retail demand is thus a blend of volume-driven and discretionary spending, with opportunities for luxury positioning in high-value segments. The tourism structure supports stable mid-market consumption but requires targeted strategies to capture high-value discretionary spend.
