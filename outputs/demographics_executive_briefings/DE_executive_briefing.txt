**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with the 25–49 cohort—critical for apparel and luxury demand—declining from 32.9% in 2016 to 31.5% in 2025. This contraction is partially offset by the 50+ segment, which grows from 37.9% to 43.4% over the same period, reflecting ageing intensity. Natural population decline (births minus deaths) worsens annually, peaking at -335,217 in 2023, while net migration fluctuates but fails to fully compensate, averaging ~300,000 annually. Projections to 2050 show minimal population growth (0.2% annual increase), suggesting long-term demand sustainability hinges on older cohorts and migration-dependent stabilization. The workforce implications are clear: labor shortages may emerge in core consumption segments, while luxury markets must adapt to ageing demographics with higher disposable income but lower growth potential.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated, with 83–86% of 25–64-year-olds holding upper secondary education, though a slight decline since 2020 signals potential income polarization. Household sizes remain stable at 2.0 members, indicating a structurally individual-driven market with smaller, sophisticated spending units. This aligns with luxury and apparel trends favoring personalized, high-value purchases over family-oriented consumption. The combination of high education levels and stable household sizes suggests a market where discretionary spending is resilient but increasingly segmented by age and income. Structural constraints may arise if educational attainment declines further, but current data supports a profile of affluent, smaller-household consumers with sophisticated preferences.
