**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with the 25–49 cohort—the core consumption segment—declining from 32.9% in 2016 to 31.5% in 2025, while the 50+ and 65+ age groups expand. The 80+ segment nearly doubles (5.8% to 7.2%), signaling intensifying ageing. Natural population decline (births minus deaths) worsens annually, reaching -330,641 in 2024, but net migration partially offsets this, averaging ~300,000 annually. Projections to 2050 indicate minimal population growth (0.1% annual increase), suggesting long-term demand sustainability hinges on migration. The workforce faces structural pressure as the 25–49 group shrinks, while ageing consumers may shift spending toward premium, health-conscious, and experiential luxury. The consumer base is thus stable but ageing, with migration mitigating but not reversing demographic decline.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated, with 83–86% of 25–64-year-olds holding upper secondary education, ensuring a sophisticated, income-capable demographic. However, a slight decline in educational attainment (86.5% in 2016 to 84.1% in 2024) may polarize income potential. Household size remains constant at 2.0, indicating smaller, individual-driven consumption patterns. This suggests a market oriented toward personalized luxury, digital engagement, and premium services rather than family-oriented spending. The structural profile is thus highly educated, smaller-household, and individual-driven, with ageing gradually introducing income polarization risks. Long-term consumption patterns will favor experiential and aspirational luxury, though workforce constraints may emerge from educational attainment trends.
