**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with the 25–49 cohort—critical for apparel and luxury demand—contracting from 32.9% in 2016 to 31.5% in 2025. This decline is partially offset by modest growth in the 0–14 and 65+ segments, reflecting a gradual ageing trajectory. Natural population decline (births minus deaths) has worsened consistently, while net migration fluctuates but fails to fully compensate for demographic losses. Projections to 2050 indicate minimal population growth, suggesting long-term demand sustainability hinges on leveraging ageing cohorts and migration-dependent segments. The workforce implications are clear: labor supply pressures may emerge as the 25–49 group shrinks, while older consumers (50+) become a larger but potentially less growth-oriented segment. The structural outlook is one of stability with ageing, requiring strategic adaptation to shifting consumption patterns.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated, with 83–86% of the 25–64 population holding upper secondary education, though a slight decline since 2020 signals potential income polarization risks. Household sizes remain stable at 2.0 members, indicating a structurally entrenched trend toward smaller, individual-driven consumption. This suggests a market skewed toward premium, personalized spending rather than family-oriented purchases. The combination of high education levels and compact households implies a sophisticated but potentially fragmented demand base, where discretionary spending on luxury and apparel may remain resilient but requires targeted segmentation. The absence of household size growth reinforces the need for strategies catering to individual preferences over collective consumption.
