**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with the 25–49 core consumption segment declining from 32.9% in 2016 to 31.5% in 2025, while the 50+ cohort expands, particularly the 80+ group, which grows from 5.8% to 7.2%. Natural population decline (births minus deaths) worsens annually, reaching -330,641 in 2024, but net migration partially offsets this, averaging 350,000 annually. Projections through 2050 show minimal population growth (0.05% annual increase), indicating a plateau rather than expansion. The ageing intensity is pronounced, with the 65+ share rising from 21.1% to 22.7% over the period. While migration mitigates contraction, the long-term demand sustainability for apparel and luxury goods is at risk due to shrinking prime-age cohorts and rising elderly dependency ratios, which may reduce workforce participation and disposable income growth.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated, with 86.5% of 25–64-year-olds holding upper secondary education in 2016, though this declines slightly to 84.1% by 2024, suggesting a stable but slightly eroding skill base. Household sizes remain constant at 2.0 members, indicating a structurally individual-driven market with smaller, more independent consumption units. This aligns with a preference for individualized spending over family-oriented purchases, reinforcing demand for premium, personalized apparel and luxury goods. The ageing population may introduce income polarization, but the educated, smaller-household structure ensures sustained sophistication in consumption patterns, albeit with potential shifts toward experiential and status-driven purchases over volume-based spending.
