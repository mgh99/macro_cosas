**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with the 25–49 core consumption cohort declining from 31.7% in 2016 to 30.3% in 2025, while the 65+ segment expands significantly, particularly the 65–79 group (rising from 13.0% to 15.7%). Natural population change has weakened sharply, dropping from 190,320 in 2016 to just 17,336 in 2024, indicating a long-term fertility decline. However, net migration has offset this contraction, with positive inflows averaging over 100,000 annually since 2017. Projections to 2050 suggest gradual population growth, but the ageing trend persists, with the 50+ share expanding. This implies sustained demand in premium and luxury segments catering to older consumers, though workforce participation may face pressure. The structural outlook is one of stability with ageing, requiring strategic adaptation to demographic shifts rather than contraction.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated and individual-driven, with upper secondary education attainment rising from 78.1% in 2016 to 83.9% in 2024, signaling higher income potential and sophistication in consumption. Average household size has remained stable at 2.1–2.2, reflecting smaller, more concentrated households. This suggests a structural shift toward individualized spending, particularly in apparel and luxury, where discretionary purchasing power is concentrated among educated, smaller households. The ageing trend may introduce income polarization, but the educated, smaller-household dynamic will likely sustain demand for premium products. The long-term implication is a market defined by high-value, experience-driven consumption rather than volume-based growth.
