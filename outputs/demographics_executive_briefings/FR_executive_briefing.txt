**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with long-term demand implications shaped by declining core consumption cohorts and persistent ageing trends. The 25–49 age group, the primary driver of apparel and luxury demand, has steadily contracted from 31.7% in 2016 to 30.3% in 2025, reflecting a shrinking workforce and peak spending demographic. Concurrently, the 50+ and 65+ cohorts have expanded, with the 65–79 group rising from 13.0% to 15.7% over the same period, signaling an ageing population with shifting consumption priorities. Natural population change has declined sharply, from 190,320 in 2016 to just 17,336 in 2024, while net migration has partially offset this decline, particularly from 2017 onward. However, migration alone cannot sustain long-term growth, as projections through 2050 indicate a gradual population increase (from 68.8 million in 2026 to 70.0 million in 2035) driven primarily by demographic inertia rather than robust expansion. The structural outlook is one of stability with ageing intensity, requiring strategic adaptation to older, more mature consumer segments while managing workforce constraints.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is structurally defined by high educational attainment and smaller households, with implications for individualized, premium-oriented spending. Upper secondary education attainment among 25–64-year-olds has risen consistently, from 78.1% in 2016 to 83.9% in 2024, indicating a skilled, income-resilient cohort with greater purchasing power and sophistication. Average household size has remained stable at 2.2 members, with a slight dip to 2.1 in 2019 and 2024, reinforcing a trend toward smaller, more independent households. This structure favors discretionary spending on luxury and apparel, as consumers prioritize quality and personalization over family-oriented purchases. The combination of high education levels and smaller households suggests a market where individual-driven consumption and premiumization will dominate, though income polarization among older cohorts may introduce segmentation challenges. The structural consumer profile is thus highly educated, smaller-household, and individual-driven, with long-term demand anchored in premiumization rather than volume growth.
