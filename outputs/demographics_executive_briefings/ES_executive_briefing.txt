**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with migration partially offsetting natural decline. The 25–49 cohort, the core consumption segment, has contracted from 37.1% in 2016 to a projected 33.1% by 2025, while the 50+ and 65+ groups have expanded, signaling an ageing population. Natural population change has been negative since 2017, with deaths outpacing births, but net migration has surged, particularly from 2018 onward, mitigating overall decline. Projections to 2050 indicate gradual population growth, though this masks underlying ageing pressures. The workforce and consumer base will face long-term sustainability challenges as the 25–49 demographic shrinks, while older cohorts grow, potentially shifting demand toward premium, health-conscious, and experiential luxury segments.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated, with upper secondary attainment rising steadily from 58.3% in 2016 to 64.9% in 2024, reflecting a skilled, income-resilient population capable of sustaining premium consumption. Household size has remained stable at 2.5 members, suggesting a balance between individual and family-oriented spending. The combination of high education levels and stable household structures implies a market driven by sophisticated, individual-driven consumption patterns, particularly in apparel and luxury goods. However, ageing may introduce income polarization, requiring targeted strategies for both affluent older consumers and younger, educated professionals.
