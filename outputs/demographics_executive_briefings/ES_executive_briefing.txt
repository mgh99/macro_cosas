**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with migration partially offsetting natural decline. The 25–49 cohort, the core consumption segment, has shrunk from 37.1% in 2016 to 33.1% in 2025, while the 50+ and 65+ groups have expanded, signaling an ageing trajectory. Natural population change has been negative since 2017, with deaths outpacing births, but net migration has surged, particularly from 2018 onward, mitigating contraction. Projections to 2050 indicate gradual population growth, though ageing intensifies, with the 65+ share rising from 18.8% in 2023 to an estimated 20.7% by 2050. This suggests long-term demand sustainability will hinge on older cohorts' spending power, while workforce dynamics may face pressure from a shrinking 25–49 demographic. The structural outlook is one of stability with ageing, not expansion or accelerated decline.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated, with upper secondary attainment rising from 58.3% in 2016 to 64.9% in 2024, reflecting higher income potential and consumption sophistication. Household size has remained constant at 2.5 members, indicating stable but not expanding family structures. This suggests a market skewed toward individual-driven spending rather than family-oriented consumption, with smaller households likely prioritizing premium, personalized apparel and luxury goods. The ageing trend, combined with rising education levels, implies a structurally sophisticated but potentially income-polarized consumer base, where discretionary spending may concentrate among higher-earning, educated segments. The absence of household size change reinforces the focus on individual consumption patterns over multi-generational purchasing.
