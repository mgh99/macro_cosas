**Dynamics & Growth Structure**
The consumer base is structurally stable but ageing, with a gradual decline in the core 25–49 consumption segment (shrinking from 37.1% in 2016 to 33.1% in 2025) and a concurrent rise in the 50+ cohort, particularly the 50–64 and 65–79 age groups. Natural population decline (consistently negative since 2017) is partially offset by net migration, which, while volatile, has been substantial in recent years (e.g., 731,768 in 2022). However, migration alone cannot sustain long-term growth, as projections indicate a plateau in total population by 2035, followed by a gradual decline. The ageing intensity is pronounced, with the 65+ share rising from 18.7% in 2016 to 20.7% in 2025. This structural shift implies a shrinking workforce and a consumer base increasingly dominated by older cohorts, which may reduce demand for luxury apparel over the medium to long term. The risk of accelerated contraction is mitigated by migration but remains a critical strategic consideration for workforce planning and market positioning.

**Socio-Economic & Household Structure**
The dominant consumer profile is highly educated, with upper secondary attainment rising steadily (from 58.3% in 2016 to 64.9% in 2024), signaling a skilled, income-resilient segment with higher consumption sophistication. However, the stable average household size (2.5 persons) suggests a market characterized by smaller, individual-driven spending patterns rather than family-oriented consumption. This aligns with an ageing population, where discretionary spending may shift toward premium, personalized luxury goods. The combination of rising education levels and smaller households implies a structurally sophisticated but potentially constrained consumer base, as ageing and income polarization could limit demand elasticity. The absence of household size changes reinforces the focus on individualized consumption, requiring targeted strategies for high-value, niche segments.
